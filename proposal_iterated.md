I think you're exactly right to stop thinking about the deck and start thinking about the **3-minute emotional arc**.

Because in a hackathon pitch, judges are not deciding:

> Is this research rigorous?

or

> Is this a complete company?

They're deciding:

> Is this team seeing something important that others are missing?

And I think you have a chance to do that.

# First: I think you're making a mistake about your background

You said:

> I feel like we may miss out on the important stuff like my experiences and the background research.

My reaction is:

**The experiences matter. The research mostly doesn't.**

Let me explain.

Nobody in the audience cares that you read Michael Smith.

Nobody cares that you read Sendhil.

Nobody cares that you read Pat.

(At least not during a 3-minute pitch.)

But they absolutely care that:

> A person who attended Harvard, Georgetown, LSE, MIT courses, online programs, private tutoring, and countless digital learning experiences discovered that almost none of those experiences were legible to the institutions allocating opportunity.

That's powerful.

Because it's a lived contradiction.

---

Imagine opening with:

> By the time I was 18, I had taken courses from Harvard, MIT, Georgetown, LSE, private tutors, and online platforms.
>
> Those experiences changed my life.
>
> None of them meaningfully changed my resume.

That is a very strong opening.

It's personal.

It's surprising.

It's memorable.

---

# Second: I think Michael Smith is your strongest intellectual foundation

Not because he's famous.

Because his paradox is excellent.

The paradox is:

> Online education won.
>
> Universities won harder.

That's a fantastic setup.

Everybody expects online learning to kill universities.

Instead:

* Stanford became more valuable.
* Harvard became more valuable.
* MIT became more valuable.

Why?

Because education was never just education.

It was trust.

---

That leads naturally to your thesis:

> The problem isn't delivering knowledge.
>
> The problem is making capability legible.

I think that's the killer line.

---

# Third: I think you're overestimating how much "solution" you need

In a 3-minute pitch, the solution should almost feel inevitable.

The audience should spend 70% of the time thinking:

> Holy shit, that's true.

And only 30% thinking:

> That's a clever solution.

Most founders invert this.

# If I Were Designing The Pitch

I would not follow the standard structure.

I'd tell a story.

Something like:

---

### 0:00 - 0:30

Personal Contradiction

> I attended Harvard, Georgetown and LSE summer schools.
>
> I completed an MIT MicroMasters.
>
> I spent years learning online.
>
> Those experiences transformed me.
>
> Yet when I applied for jobs, almost none of them mattered.

Pause.

> That's when I realized something strange.

---

### 0:30 - 1:00

The Michael Smith Paradox

> We thought online education would disrupt universities.
>
> Instead universities became stronger than ever.
>
> Why?
>
> Because education isn't the scarce thing.
>
> Trust is.

Now you've got them.

---

### 1:00 - 1:30

The Insight

> Today we measure capability using credentials.
>
> But credentials are compressed summaries of learning.
>
> AI allows us to observe learning itself.

This is where you introduce:

* curiosity
* adaptation
* learning velocity
* conceptual transfer

Not as features.

As evidence.

---

### 1:30 - 2:30

Demo

This is the heart.

The entire demo should answer one question:

> Can we learn something meaningful about a person from how they learn?

Nothing else.

No dashboards.

No complexity.

No settings.

No accounts.

---

You show:

Student A.

Student B.

Same outcome.

Different learning traces.

Then:

> Traditional education says these students are identical.
>
> Our system doesn't.

That's the magical moment.

---

### 2:30 - 3:00

Vision

Not:

> We built an AI tutor.

Instead:

> Universities became powerful because they were trusted capability filters.
>
> We believe AI can create a new way to recognize human potential.
>
> Not by measuring what people know.
>
> But by understanding how they learn.

Done.

# The MVP

Now let's talk practically.

If I had 24 hours, I would build something much narrower than we've discussed.

The MVP should not attempt to:

* predict careers
* build scholarships
* replace universities
* discover founders

That's all too far away.

Instead:

## Goal

Convince someone that learning behavior contains hidden information.

---

I'd create a learning experience around a novel topic.

Maybe:

* an invented language
* a strange puzzle system
* a made-up symbolic domain

The key is:

**Nobody starts with prior knowledge.**

That removes a massive confounder.

---

Then I'd track:

* time spent
* retries
* question quality
* adaptation speed

And produce:

### Traditional View

Score: 8/10

### Capability View

* Rapid abstraction
* Strong transfer
* High curiosity
* Moderate persistence

The actual labels don't matter much.

The contrast does.

---

# The Question I'm Thinking About

I think the most important thing for us to figure out next is:

**What is the single most surprising thing the demo can reveal about a learner in under 60 seconds?**

Not the most accurate.

Not the most scientifically valid.

The most surprising.

Because that's the moment judges will remember.

For example:

> "This learner learns entirely through analogy."

or

> "This learner asks questions that resemble domain experts."

or

> "These two learners got the same score but one learned the concept 3x faster."

I suspect the entire demo should be built around one such revelation.

If we can identify that moment, the deck almost writes itself. That's where I'd focus our next round of thinking.
